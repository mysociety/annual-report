It's been a year like no other

Yup, that's hardly an original thought.

Fortunately for us, mySociety's Annual Report comes out before everyone else's, so we can get in first with the clichés. Yes, these are unprecedented times. The pandemic wrought havoc and plunged us into a new normal.

There have been upheavals, disruptions to our plans. Travel disallowed; face to face meetings largely a thing of the past — the biggest work-related disappointment of lockdown, for us, was the cancellation of our Reykjavik TICTeC conference.

There again, in many ways we have been fortunate. We've experienced little operational disturbance, since we've always been a remote organisation where everyone works from home. Whilst we've all felt the stress of the pandemic over the last few months, it's possible we've been more productive than usual without the interruption of travel.

And so, our big changes this year? Well, they have nothing to do with COVID and everything to do with our core mission as an organisation: helping people be active citizens.

Digital democracy goes global

With the Westminster Foundation for Democracy and the United Nations Development Programme, we've worked with parliaments in Myanmar, Morocco, Armenia, Bhutan, Pakistan and Timor-Leste as they undergo the process of digitalisation.

Putting parliamentary services online has never been more urgent — and, aptly enough, most of the work had to be conducted remotely.

/*[Bec/Zarino photos]*/

As a designer, it was fascinating seeing how another country's parliament works, and the challenges faced by MPs and their support staff, in representing a citizenry with completely different economic and technological challenges to anywhere else I'd seen.

Work we did with the Moroccan parliament demonstrated that citizens there preferred some of the changes to parliamentary activity made as a result of COVID-19, especially the move to broadcast parliamentary sessions on TV.

This has enabled citizens to inform themselves and better hold their parliamentarians to account, and we have recommended that these broadcasts are continued beyond the pandemic.

mySociety life in lockdown

Migrating all of our digital transformation work with international parliaments to remote-only activity has been really difficult. It is so much harder to build rapport with people, get a sense of how people use technology, and how the digital and physical space in parliaments overlaps, when you are only able to speak to people via Skype. It is made even more difficult when you need simultaneous translation to communicate.

What's been the most fun about Democracy this year?

Giving oral evidence to the Lords Committee on Digital Democracy back in March was quite rewarding, as was seeing many of our recommendations included in the final report. Digital democracy is kind of our thing, so it is always good to have that recognised by the people who govern us!

Climate

... we're working on some of the tools you need to fight climate breakdown.

What we did in 2020

We started 2020 with a commitment to the climate.

As that includes, of course, a look at our own activities, we formed the mySociety Climate Action Group, open for any staff member to join. Together, they create new policies and assess the improvements we can make.

Some of the things we've done so far (find more detail in our environment policy):

  • Made a decision to offset not just our travel, but all our carbon emissions
  • Investigated the worth of offset schemes, deciding to make our own offsets directly
  • Restricted business travel (once that sort of thing is permitted again, that is)
  • Joined Climate Perks to incentivise sustainable holiday travel
  • Extended the life of our hardware
  • Assessed third party suppliers on their climate commitments
  • Calculated our existing carbon footprint, so we'll know how much progress we're making
  • Committed to making the climate a primary consideration when renewing our hosting contracts

...and there's plenty more to come next year.

Who we worked with...

In January, we were able to start making good on our commitment to help those fighting climate change, providing the website for the UK's national Climate Assembly.

We were part of the team supporting over 100 citizens who gave up their time to inform the UK's response to the climate crisis as part of Climate Assembly UK - a huge contribution of active citizenship

And, with local councils up and down the country declaring a Climate Emergency, we thought it was vital to start collating their Action Plans alongside climateemergency.uk. Watch this space as we make that dataset more useful for researchers, journalists, campaigners and citizens.

What did you do with our stuff?

On a similar note, Friends of the Earth created this toolwith our MapIt API, allowing you to look up how climate-friendly your neighbourhood is.

And then there was TICTeC

TICTeC 2020 ¬∑ Virtual event ¬∑ 24th‚Äì25th March

We'd planned our annual Impacts of Civic Tech conference as usual: we'd have been convening in Reykjavik, reconnecting with friends old and new from around the world.

In March. And we all know what happened to March.

And so, it was a quick turnaround time: as with so many in-person events, large and small, we had to take it online (in just ten days!). We still heard from a range of knowledgeable and inspiring speakers‚Ä¶ but they were presenting from their own homes rather than the TICTeC podium. Around 250 people from over 30 different countries attended; and speakers hailed from all continents across the world.

Nanjala Nyabola's keynote session felt more intimate, even as she was giving us a sweeping global perspective on how the internet era is transforming politics.

{% include image.html src="https://place-hold.it/540x350" width="540" height="350" alt="" loading="lazy" class="card-img-top" %}

And our fireside chat with our other keynote speaker, Dr. Hollie Russon Gilman, highlighted that the pandemic is an important opportunity to reimagine how civic tools can be used in a way to build civic power.

Other notable TICTeC sessions included Civic Hall presenting their latest research on how organisations have managed to survive, and even thrive, in the difficult field of civic engagement, social change, and the digital transformation of democracy.

Neema Iyer from Pollicy presented their large-scale research study on the prevalence of online-based violence against women across five countries in Africa, and how this affects their access to civic technology.

Sruti Modekurty from OpenAQ talked about how their work making access to air quality data openly available has had a real impact on influencing policy, and ultimately improved the health of citizens.

These examples, and the others we heard at TICTeC 2020, are just the kind of evidence-based impact and research stories we love to hear at TICTeC events, to inform the future development of civic technology.
