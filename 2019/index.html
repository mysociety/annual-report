mySociety in 2019

{% include image.html src="/assets/img/mark.jpg" loading="lazy" width="200" height="200" class="intro-mark-photo rounded-circle mr-3 mr-sm-0" alt="Mark Cridge" %} Mark Cridge, CEO

Welcome to our annual report. As always, it’s a chance to catch up with everything we’ve been doing in the last twelve months — and to take stock.

We’ve been thinking deeply about what part mySociety has to play in the wider picture of global and national events. It’s been a heck of a year, with the shattering of democratic norms; the increasing grip of deep fakes, misdirection and misinformation; the growing international recognition of climate tipping points, and failed promises from those in whom we most need to lay our trust.

What gives us hope is that the tides have turned: the recognition that it is possible for ordinary people to hold power to account, to organise effectively for change, and to confront the challenges that lie ahead. Now, more than ever, it’s vital that every citizen feels empowered to act.

And whether you're delighted or dismayed by the UK General Election result, one thing’s for sure: mySociety will go on providing the resolutely non-partisan services that we’re known for, through 2020 and beyond. We’ll help you hold your representatives to account, demand transparency and make change in your own neighbourhood.

In short, we’ll be here to help you in every aspect of your life as an active citizen.

TICTeC

The world’s only conference exploring the impacts of Civic Technology

First, at the OECD in Paris

TICTeC 2019 • Château de la Muette, Paris • 19th–20th March

The Civic Tech community came together in Paris… in the springtime. Someone should write a song about that.

200 attendees

29 countries

76 speakers

??? hours of video

200 people from 29 countries congregated in the OECD’s gilded chateau, to hear 76 speakers present, debate, and grapple with experiences from the coalfaces of Civic Tech around the world.

Alessandra Orofino inspired everyone as she recounted experiences of tackling ‘platform politicians’ of the far right. Takeaway quote? “The next generation will save democracy if we let them”.

James Anderson from Bloomberg Philanthropies gave examples of local governments making innovative, imaginative decisions against a “crisis of legitimacy that is unprecedented within our generation”.

We heard the French perspective from Deputy Mayor of Paris Pauline Véron, MP Paula Forteza and Tatiana de Feraudy from Decider ensemble, demonstrating how Civic Tech in the shape of the Grand Débat National offered a response to the big social challenge raised by the gilets jaunes.

And then, at City Hall in London

TICTeC Local 2019 • City Hall, London • 1st November

TICTeC Local occupied the top floor of the state-of-the-art, snail-shaped City Hall on London’s South Bank.

We talked communities and local government, with the climate crisis top of everyone’s minds. Are Citizens’ Assemblies an adequate response? What can they achieve… and what are the potential pitfalls?

There were uplifting stories of the power of civic tech to empower residents, make food banks more effective, turn impoverished communities around, and help the homeless.

What was your memorable TICTeC moment?

Gemma It was amazing to be hosted by Paula Forteza MP and Mounir Mahjoubi (Secretary of State for Digital Affairs) at the French National Assembly building — and to hear and feel their support for the civic tech movement.

Myf Seeing a young female holding the whole room captivated by her message. Brava, Alessandra Orofino.

Transparency

We focused on: supporting the users of WhatDoTheyKnowPro; fostering the community of people who use and run FOI sites around the world; working with people in authorities who deal with FOI requests; reporting the amazing things people have done with their right to information; and researching FOI from a variety of angles.

What we made

We put out a new release for all those running FOI sites on the Alaveteli platform.

Thanks to funding from Adessium Foundation, we’re working with partners across Europe to install Alaveteli Pro on their Alaveteli sites, allowing for more investigative journalism projects using FOI as a tool.

What people did with it

In the UK…

xxx people used WhatDoTheyKnow to make 26,000 requests this year, while more than 6.5 million visited the site to browse the information released in responses.

We covered some of the exceptional stories that came from FOI requests made through the site:

  • How schoolchildren’s data was used to support the “hostile environment”
  • Uncovering a report on the implications of an Irish border
  • A UK-wide audit of the public buildings and land sold by councils under the pressures of austerity
  • Release of research supplied to the pro-Brexit group ERG
  • Tracing the shortcomings of the support system for those caught up in modern slavery

…and around the world

Ma Dada brought simple FOI requesting to France, on the Alaveteli platform…

…while TuNa Bakonzi did the same for the Republic of Congo…

…and VreauInfo is up and running in Moldova…

…plus, this just in, Wob Knop is now running Alaveteli Pro for professional users of FOI in the Netherlands.

This brings the total number of active FOI sites being run around the world on our Alaveteli software to … 27!

What we discovered

  • Working with Hackney, Suffolk, Stevenage, East Herts and Cornwall, we studied the workflows of council staff who manage and respond to FOI and Subject Access requests, and published our research findings.
  • How many FOI requests are received each year in local government? This piece of research delved into just that.
  • And meanwhile, how many of the FOI requests going to central government come out of our site WhatDoTheyKnow? We look into the figures here.
  • We travelled to sub-Saharan Africa to look into Legal Information Institutes and what they do to help everyone gain access to the law.

Who we met

AlaveteliCon in Oslo brought together around 50 journalists, researchers, technologists and activists from 18 different countries, all with an interest in the use of FOI to investigate stories and run campaigns.

We heard about suing Parliament; cat and mouse games with bureaucracy; the alarming tech increasingly deployed by the police; how AI can have unintended effects on everything from school bus routes to bail conditions; the clandestine graves of Mexico and many many more innovative ways in which FOI has been used to uncover vital stories.

Lovely to spend time with our US counterparts Muckrock as well!

Democracy

We focused on: How tech could support the increasingly favoured civic phenomenon of Citizens’ Assemblies; refining TheyWorkForYou and making sure it survives into the next year; working closely with councils to get more people engaged in democracy at a local level.

How you helped

The TheyWorkForYou crowdfunder has a week to run. There’s still time to donate and help us keep the site going as we work to get everything in order post-election — and, we hope, add some new features to help people understand Parliament even more clearly.

Of course you do!

Here’s the link.

What we discovered

  • We delved deep into what should be considered when creating a website for a Citizens’ Assembly…
  • …part of wider research into whether digital tools should be used when running them, and if so, which ones, and how?
  • And we published a series of posts looking at how our own WriteToThem site is used.

What we made

We built the website for Parliament’s forthcoming Climate Assembly, supporting the work of Involve and Sortition Foundation.

Through Public Square we helped the Democratic Society support the work of three councils – Frome, Glasgow and Calderdale – to explore participatory methods of Democracy.

We added graphics to TheyWorkForYou to make votes easier to understand at a glance.

And now, as well as the UK, Scottish and Northern Ireland parliaments, you can also follow the London Assembly on TheyWorkForYou!

What people did with it

More than 3.5 million people used TheyWorkForYou to check out who their MP was, see how they voted and browse parliamentary debates.

More than half a million folk used WritetoThem to send an email to their councillors, MPs, Assembly Members or MEPs.

TheyWorkForYou and MapIt’s data helps others to create visualisations and conduct research, like this:

Parli-N-Grams, charting the frequency at which words and phrases are used in UK Parliament debates. Sometimes these are words which a certain Member of Parliament would prefer not to encounter.

Tactical voting advice was potentially useful — but what if every service advocates a different approach? Charles Owen tried to work it all out.

Voters could see and share electoral facts and margins at a glance thanks to these constituency cards.

Countless news providers used TheyWorkForYou to underpin stories like these:

The New York Times linked to a TheyWorkForYou debate page in a story about Boris Johnson’s friendship with an entrepreneur who benefited from mayoral and governmental sponsorship.

CarbonBrief used our data when analysing which MPs talk the most about climate change.

ForcesNetwork published a piece on the new defence minister’s voting record; RightsInfo looked at the Tory leadership contenders in terms of their human rights stances; Pink News checked Jo Swinson’s voting records on gay rights when she became leaader of the Lib Dems, and Country Life examined the new Environment minister’s record.

The Independent profiled Penny Morduant when she became Theresa May’s new defence secretary.

The Week compared Boris Johnson’s voting record to the pledges he made in his first week as PM.

Newsletter, the Belfast paper, pointed to Lord Trimble’s voting record on equal gay rights in a story on his daughter’s same sex marriage.

Many organisations used WriteToThem to help with campaigns, including: Extinction Rebellion, End Deportations, League Against Cruel Sports, Humanity and Inclusion, Where’s Your Head At, and both pro- and anti-Brexit campaigns.

Birthday!

TheyWorkForYou turned 15 (that said, it displays the Hansard archive as far back as 1919).

Who we worked with

We joined Westminster Foundation for Democracy in a visit to Uzbekistan’s Parliament in Tashkent, helping to analyse their current digital work, and making recommendations on how to open up their parliamentary processes digitally to increase citizen awareness and engagement.

We successfully handed parliamentary monitoring sites the People's Assembly (South Africa) and Mzalendo (Kenya) over to full local control, a great testament to the advanced capability of the civic tech sector in sub-Saharan Africa.

Where we said goodbye

Our involvement with EveryPolitician comes to an end, as our ability to continue the project without sufficient dedicated funding has diminished – we’ve lots to learn and share for other organisations about what we might do differently in the future.

Community

We worked on: rolling out new features for FixMyStreet Pro client councils; refining online mapping of community spaces and places; setting out a way forward for open data in the government’s planning register; prototyping a service to empower people who live in tower blocks.

What we discovered

  • We made recommendations around planning applications — specifically towards sharing open data.
  • We worked with Tower Blocks UK to discover and prototype digital tools that would help to empower high rise residents.
  • Our Research department looked more deeply into the effect of gender on FixMyStreet reports
  • And they also did a lot of work around FixMyStreet and the different demographics that use it.

What we made

Keep It In The Community, our site that maps Assets of Community Value and other places significant to communities gained gorgeous new features.

[…]

What people did with it

You made more than 387,000 FixMyStreet reports this year, either on FixMyStreet.com, via the app, or on one of the 22 council sites that use FixMyStreet Pro as their main reporting interface.

Saving money

Buckinghamshire County Council totted up the precise savings they’ve made since installing FixMyStreet Pro as their official reporting system. A 98.69% cut in costs? Now that’s impressive.

Saving the trees

Friends of the Earth recommended FixMyStreet as a way to protect local trees.

Fixing Grenada

A new site based on the FixMyStreet platform, for the “Island of Spice”.

Who we worked with

Thanks to our new partnership with TfL, there’s a whole new layer of connectivity on FixMyStreet for Greater London.

Reports on the relevant bus stops, traffic lights, bus shelters, underground and overground stations are now routed direct to TFL to get them fixed.

Building our FixMyStreet Pro business

Running commercial services for local government means we can bring better, more efficient workflows to council staff and officers.

The revenue also supports our charitable work, providing a significant and appropriate source of income.

Over 6.5 million residents across several counties are enjoying easy street reporting direct from their councils’ websites, thanks to beautiful new FixMyStreet instances in…

  • Northamptonshire
  • Hounslow
  • Westminster
  • Isle of Wight
  • Peterborough
  • Bexley

Making our data more accessible

Working with folk from the University of Sheffield and University of Stirling, we decided how best to share FixMyStreet open data with researchers.
