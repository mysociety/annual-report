{% include image.html src="/assets/img/mark-cridge.jpg" loading="lazy" width="200" height="200" class="intro-mark-photo rounded-circle mr-3 mr-sm-0" alt="Mark Cridge" %} Mark Cridge, Chief Executive

We’ve learned to live with a lot of things in 2021. Against a backdrop of a neverending pandemic, hard won rights are under threat; protest is being criminalised; and action to combat the climate crises benefits from lots of fine words but little real change in behaviour.

At mySociety we see our work as being more important than ever; helping people be active as citizens, holding power to account, and understanding how decisions are made on our behalf by our elected representatives. In this annual review for 2021 we’ll celebrate the really powerful and exciting work we’ve been able to put together over the year, the amazing staff and volunteer team that allows us to do this, and the millions of people that benefit from using our services.

Faced with the scale of the challenges we are all facing together, we’ve realised that we need to make some significant changes to how we operate in the future.

We recently set out some thinking about the shifts we need to make in how we work, and why we think that, as a society, we need to Repower Democracy.

Repowering democracy means finding better ways to collectively tackle societal level problems; creating supportive spaces for our leaders to make the difficult decisions that will be needed in years to come. It means improving the legitimacy and effectiveness of representative democracy so that it’s better able to embrace participatory democracy — creating the conditions for citizen and community power to thrive and flourish. There is a lot to be done.

So, enjoy hearing about what we’ve been up to in 2021, and we look forward to seeing how we can repower democracy with you in 2022.

Words from mySociety’s Chair

{% include image.html src="/assets/img/catherine.jpg" loading="lazy" width="100" height="100" class="mt-5 rounded-circle d-inline-block mr-3 mr-sm-0" alt="Catherine Brown" %} Catherine Brown,
Board member

What a year — for the world and for mySociety.

It has been a busy year for mySociety and the first thing I want to say is a huge “thank you” to the team. As you can see from the contents of this review, they have done an amazing job – continuing to support and develop the services that so many people rely on, while also forging a new strategy to make sure we can make an even bigger impact in the future.

I can’t choose just one favourite highlight for the year – there are just too many great things going on. The work to help people follow through on WhatDoTheyKnow queries, and stop organisations using the exemptions wrongly to brush off Freedom of Information requests, is certainly one favourite. And the work with our partner Climate Emergency UK to make local authorities’ Climate Action Plans accessible to citizens and enable councils to learn from each other is another. And then there are the 7.8 million visits to TheyWorkForYou – helping thousands of people every week understand and use the levers to influence politicians…

One of the themes of our new strategy is collaboration – the problems facing our democracy and the world are much too big and complex for any organisation to tackle on its own. Over the next year we will be working hard to combine our knowledge and skills with those of others to make a bigger difference. Our resources are stretched very thinly and it will be a priority to address that and make sure that we look after our people and focus our work where, working with others, we can make the biggest difference.

Thanks for reading our Review. If you can see ways we could work together to help reboot democracy and save the planet, please get in touch.

Climate

As it has become a priority for the world, climate has also become a priority for mySociety. Not to say that our Climate programme replaces our work on Transparency, Community and Democracy – it’s more that we’re leveraging climate opportunities within each of those streams.

What we did in 2021

{% include image.html src="/assets/img/cape-data.jpg" webp="/assets/img/cape-data.webp" width="540" height="250" alt="Climate Action Plan Explorer" loading="lazy" class="border-bottom" %}

This year, in collaboration with Climate Emergency UK, we published CAPE: our searchable database of council climate action plans. We’re continuing to add new useful features to it, that’ll help you engage with your local authority over their plans for Net Zero.

{% include image.html src="/assets/img/cape-hero.jpg" webp="/assets/img/cape-hero.webp" width="540" height="250" alt="Net Zero Local Hero" loading="lazy" class="border-bottom" %}

If you’re a citizen who cares about the climate, here’s where to begin.

Plus we know CAPE’s going to be useful for journalists, researchers, council climate officers and councillors, and the global civic tech community, too.

CAPE features and research

There’s loads more to come, but for now here are the features we’ve steadily been adding to the basic database.

We’ve published every Climate Action Plan we could find.

We’ve added the date that each council declared a climate emergency.

We’ve extracted the headline promise from every plan and displayed it on the page for that council.

{% include image.html src="/assets/img/cape-pledges-standalone.jpg" webp="/assets/img/cape-pledges-standalone.webp" width="540" height="350" alt="Pledges on CAPE" loading="lazy" %}

We’ve pulled in emissions data from the Department of Business, Energy and Skills.

We’ve added a basic API so developers can use the action plans data in their own sites and apps (we’ll be making this more sophisticated next year).

We’ve added infoboxes so everyone can understand which councils are responsible for what sort of climate action

{% include image.html src="/assets/img/power-and-responsibilities.jpg" webp="/assets/img/power-and-responsibilities.webp" width="540" height="350" alt="Infoboxes containing the powers and power and responsibilties for every council" loading="lazy" %}

And it’s also possible to download the whole database in one ZIP

And, to help councils learn from other authorities facing similar climate challenges, we’ve identified which have similar emissions profiles.

Watch this space for new additions to CAPE in 2022 – sign up to our newsletter and we’ll keep you informed

We asked the team…

What’s your top tip for helping the climate?

Try to buy things that add value to your life; it will help reduce the amount of waste we create.

Make a conscious effort not to buy things that have to be flown half way around the world before they reach you. Shop local!

Before you use a commercial service, ask to see their environmental policy – this shows there’s a competitive advantage to doing the right thing and a consumer demand for companies that care.

Find your local repair cafe and learn to med the belongings that you might otherwise have thrown away.

The facts and figures

Our UK parliament monitoring site TheyWorkForYou saw almost 8 million visits in 2021.

Peaks in traffic reflected the biggest news of the year: the murder of David Amess MP on 15th October, the debate and vote on MP Standards (including Owen Paterson MP’s conduct) on 4th November, and debates and votes on both the Domestic Abuse Bill and the Police, Crime, Sentencing and Courts Bill on 16th March.

WriteToThem served over 2,737,684 visits, and sent over 188,000 emails.

While we don’t look at the content of emails sent through WriteToThem (except very rarely, under specific circumstances as recorded in our Privacy Policy), the highest daily numbers of visits do coincide with big events in the news: The end of COP26 and the taxi explosion outside Liverpool Women’s hospital (15th November), the start of COP26, where Boris Johnson sat next to Sir David Attenborough and didn’t wear a face mask (3rd November), and the dissent over policing at the vigil for Sarah Everard (15th March).
